Its composition being the same as natural gas enables meaningful emissions reduction while continuing to make use of existing assets.

What is Biomethane?

Biomethane, also known as Renewable Natural Gas (RNG) refers to an almost pure-methane gas obtained from "upgrading" biogas. It is physically indistinguishable from natural gas, differing as it is produced through green methods.

When compared with other renewable energy sources, biomethane is dispatchable, avoiding the pitfalls of solar and wind energy. This enables it to replace natural gas - completely or within a blend, especially for industrial equipment and infrastructure, including existing gas turbine engines.

Definition: Dispatchable Energy

Energy that can be dispatched to users as required.

Biomethane production can be tailored for many places globally, with various organic feedstocks being suitable for its production. These production methods contribute to establishing a circular economy, where waste feed-stocks from other sectors are reused.

Biomethane Constitution

Biomethane is made through upgrading biogas. This process is crucial for transforming biogas into a natural gas resembling product.

Biogas is the product of anaerobic digestion. It consists of mainly methane (45-75% by volume), with the remainder being carbon dioxide and other anaerobic digestion product gases.

Biomethane on the other hand is made through removing the CO2 and other gases from this biogas.

Biomethane Emissions

Biomethane production reduces the greenhouse effect by capturing methane that would otherwise be released to the atmosphere, where it would exert a strong short-term warming impact due to its high Global Warming Potential (GWP).

Additionally, biomethane is cleaner than natural gas, as it forms part of the biogenic cycle. This means that no new emissions are being added to the atmosphere.

Biomethane Production

Biochemical Processes: Anaerobic Digestion

  • Accounts for the majority of global biomethane production.
  • Anaerobic digestion uses naturally occurring bacteria to break down organic material in an oxygen-deprived environment.
  • The resulting biogas can then be upgraded to biomethane or renewable natural gas (RNG).

Thermochemical Processes: Gasification

  • Involves breaking down woody or solid biomass at high temperatures and pressures under low-oxygen conditions.
  • This process converts biomass into a mixture of gases, primarily carbon monoxide, hydrogen, and carbon dioxide.
  • A subsequent methanation step uses a catalyst to produce methane.

Conditioning/Upgrading

For many equipment and vehicles, the quality of biomethane used must be more strictly controlled. The process of conditioning/upgrading involves removal of water, CO2, hydrogen sulphide, and other trace elements.

Biogas upgrading can be performed with several technologies, including: Water Scrubbing, Organic Solvent Scrubbing, Amine Scrubbing, Pressure Swing Adsorption (PSA), and Gas Separation Membranes.

Storage

Biomethane can be stored using a range of established and emerging gas storage technologies. These approaches are largely aligned with conventional natural gas storage practices, allowing biomethane to be stored using well-understood methods and infrastructure from across the gas industry.

Key Biomethane Storage Considerations

Initial Costs

The upfront capital expenditure required to design, construct, and commission the storage facility, including vessels, caverns, compressors, control systems, and associated infrastructure.

Maintenance Costs

The ongoing operational expenditure associated with inspection, testing, repair, and upkeep of the storage system to ensure continued safety, reliability, and regulatory compliance.

Turnover Rate / Ratio

The number of times the usable working gas volume of a storage facility can be fully injected and withdrawn over the course of a year, indicating how frequently the storage can be cycled.

Storage Capacity

The total volume of gas that can be held within the storage facility, typically divided into working gas and cushion (base) gas, and expressed in energy or volumetric terms.

Injection Rate

The maximum rate at which gas can be introduced into the storage system under defined operating conditions, influenced by pressure, temperature, and infrastructure constraints.

Withdrawal Rate

The maximum rate at which gas can be extracted from storage while maintaining safe operating pressures and flow stability, often a critical factor during high-demand periods.

Cushion Gas (Base Gas)

The volume of gas permanently retained within the storage facility to maintain sufficient pressure for effective injection and withdrawal; this gas is generally non-recoverable.

Biomethane Storage Options

Underground Storage (Salt Cavern)

Underground

The biomethane storage method with the largest volume holding capacity. There are three main underground storage methods: depleted oil and gas reservoirs, salt caverns, and aquifers.

  • Salt Caverns: Smaller capacities than oil and gas reservoirs, but provide higher gas injection and withdrawal rates, and minimal leakage. High turnover rate suitable for integration with intermittent renewable sources.
  • Oil and Gas Reservoir: Low turnover rates, typically operated on an annual seasonal-based cycle. Simpler than other below-ground methods with lower investment costs.
  • Aquifers: Rarely used for gas storage due to limited tightness, low turnover-rates, and the geological nature of aquifers.
Compressed Biomethane Storage Cylinders

Compressed Biomethane (CBM)

  • Various different options exist for CBM, including: spherical, cylindrical, pipe tanks, and gas bottles.
  • Due to pressures involved, CBM storage facilities require adequate safety devices, such as: rupture disks, pressure relief valves.
  • These devices can excel with fast filling times.
  • The main cost associated with CBM is the cost of compression.
  • Safety considerations can make identifying suitable sites for CBM storage more difficult.
Liquefied Biomethane Tanker Ship

Liquefied Biomethane (LBM)

  • LBM like LNG involves cooling methane gas to -162°C. It is very space efficient, taking up 1/600th of the volume gaseous biomethane would.
  • Heat leakage into tank can severely reduce storage efficiency by vaporizing the LBM. Boil-off complicates LBM transportation and on-site maintenance.
  • Improved safety due to non-explosive and non-toxic process. However, the cold temperatures pose unique risks.
Material Adsorption Molecular Structure

Material Adsorption - Adsorbed Biomethane (ABM)

  • An emerging technology, which allows a material to adsorb significant amount of biomethane at lower pressures than more well-established methods.
  • Enhanced safety due to reduced risk of fires, explosions, and lower pressures.
  • The various challenges concerning ABM include: managing heat during adsorption process, inability to connect to high pressure devices, and the high costs associated with ABM materials.

Main Transportation Modes

Pipelines

  • Dedicated biomethane pipelines may be used close to the point of production.
  • Existing natural gas pipeline networks can also be utilised, subject to agreements with the pipeline owner.
  • LNG or LBM pipelines may be employed in some cases; however, these systems are more complex and costly to construct and operate.
🚚

Road

  • Road transport is used where pipeline connection is impractical or not economically viable.
  • Due to biomethane's low density at ambient conditions, the gas must be compressed or converted to a liquid for transport.
  • Road transport is generally not a long-term, cost-effective solution and is more suited to temporary or transitional applications.
  • LBM is transported at relatively low pressures but requires cryogenic temperatures, meaning strict safety and handling procedures are essential.
🚢

Tankers

  • LNG or LBM tankers are primarily used for long-distance or intercontinental transport.
  • In these scenarios, pipeline transport is impractical, making marine transport the preferred option.

General Biomethane Safety

It is important to understand the fundamental safety considerations when working with biomethane

Biomethane Safety Considerations

Biomethane possesses key dangers which must be considered due to its physical and chemical properties. Because these properties closely align with those of natural gas (if not identical), existing industry knowledge, standards, and practices remain largely applicable.

Highly Flammable

Highly Flammable

Methane forms flammable mixtures with air over a relatively wide concentration range, meaning even small leaks can present an ignition risk if an ignition source is present.

Lighter Than Air

Lighter Than Air

Because methane is lighter than air, it tends to rise and accumulate in enclosed or poorly ventilated spaces such as overhead structures.

Colourless Gas

Colourless Gas

Methane cannot be detected visually at normal pressures and temperatures, which means leaks may go unnoticed without dedicated gas detection systems in place.

Naturally Odourless

Naturally Odourless

In its natural state methane has no smell, requiring the addition of odorants and the use of gas monitors to ensure leaks can be detected by personnel.

Visible Flame

Visible Flame

Biomethane flames are visible, but their pale, low-luminosity nature means they can be difficult to see in well-lit or outdoor environments.

Production Method Safety

The production process of biomethane comes with several inherent risks. Firstly, there are unique biological compounds used throughout the production process, which exposes workers to risks not commonly associated in other natural gas infrastructure. Additionally, many common risks associated with natural gas and hydrogen production are present.

Phases of Biogas/Biomethane Plant Operations

Normal Operations

The phase where the plant is continuously operating. During this phase, the plant is expected to function within design parameters. There are still many dangers anticipated within this phase, as mentioned throughout this quadrant.

The most dangerous phases of work in a biogas plant are:

Maintenance Work

Only people with relevant experience, knowledge, and qualifications should operate on biogas/biomethane facilities. Appropriate safety measures should be established, and a supervisor appointed.

Start-Up/Commissioning

During the start-up phase, there may be air contained within the previously dormant equipment. As biogas begins to flow through the equipment, flammable conditions may form. For this reason, adequate purging with biogas must take place before equipment like a downstream blower or flares can be turned on.

Shut-down

The shutdown involves several risks, which arise from the change in conditions, and failure to adhere to correct protocol. For any shutdown of a biogas/biomethane facility, ensure that:

  • Electrical Power is fully shut-off to the system
  • Pipes and system are checked for areas of flammable gases, that haven't been fully vented
  • At the time of shutdown/decommissioning, there may still be input and digestate materials present. Caution should be exercised when emptying digester vessels.

Biogas/Biomethane Plant Safety

Biogas and biomethane plants present various hazards that must be carefully managed to ensure the safety of workers and the surrounding environment.

Biological Agents

Biological Agents

Intake of biological agents through the respiratory tract (including moulds, bacteria, and endotoxins), hand-to-mouth contact, skin/mucous membrane contact, cuts.

Hazardous Substances

Hazardous Substances

May include biogas, processing aids, oils, slurries, and any silage effluent.

Electrical Hazards

Electrical Hazards

Any electrical components used in the biogas/biomethane set up that can cause electric shocks, magnetic/electric fields - especially dangerous to people with pacemakers.

Mechanical Hazards

Mechanical Hazards

Any dangers involving falling, impact, crushing, and cutting. Several locations, such as silos can involve working at heights, or in the vicinity of rotating parts or moving vehicles.

Gas Hazards

Gas Hazards

Biogas contains different gases to varying degrees. There are general exposure limits to various gases published for expected working periods (generally 8 hours a day, 5 days a week, for a working lifetime).

Explosion and Fire Hazards

Explosion and Fire Hazards

Flammable substances may be present in the form of gases, vapours, mists or dusts. Explosions can only occur if there is a flammable substance (in required distribution and concentration), oxygen, and a source of ignition.

Explosive Reignition

A pipeline-fueled fire should never be extinguished unless the pipeline has been isolated.

If a fire has been extinguished, but the gas pipeline isn't isolated, gas can migrate until it finds another ignition source. This is termed 'explosive re-ignition', and can endanger lives.

⚠️

Pipeline Dangers

  • There is a risk of damage to pipeline infrastructure during excavation/maintenance works.
  • Pipeline ruptures can be caused by mechanical impacts, including those from natural events such as earthquakes.
⚠️

Road Transportation Dangers

⚠️

Road Transportation Dangers

  • Trucks face risks from road accidents. For gas transportation vehicles, minor collisions may have significant consequences.
  • In the case of an LNG transportation vehicle accident, the extremely cold LNG will initially sink to the ground.
  • For CNG vehicle accidents, the gas will naturally rise. In enclosed spaces, there could be an increased risk of ignition.
  • Sparks and static electricity from friction between vehicle components and tanks must be controlled with proper grounding.
  • Human error remains a major factor; thorough training and strict adherence to safety procedures are essential.
⚠️

Tanker Dangers

⚠️

Tanker Dangers

  • In enclosed spaces like ships, natural gas leaks can cause asphyxiation by displacing oxygen.
  • The cryogenic conditions involved with LNG storage can cause embrittlement of several materials.
  • In LNG tankers, double-hull designs are employed for safety against collisions.
  • LNG itself will not burn. However, the vaporised gases from LNG can ignite if an ignition source is present.
